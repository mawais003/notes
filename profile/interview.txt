💬 **Question**: Explain the difference between point-in-time recovery and other database recovery methods

---

⭐️ **Answer**: Point-in-time recovery (PITR) is a database recovery method that allows you to restore the database to a specific moment in time, usually just before a failure or data corruption event. This is different from a full backup restore, which restores the database to the state at the time the backup was taken. PITR uses continuous transaction logs or write-ahead logs to replay changes up to the desired point, enabling precise recovery without losing all data after the last backup. This method is crucial for minimizing data loss and downtime in production environments, and I have implemented PITR strategies in AWS RDS databases to ensure high availability and quick recovery during incidents.

💬 **Question**: Difference between recovery and replication, and when each is useful

---

⭐️ **Answer**: Recovery and replication serve different purposes in database management. Recovery, such as point-in-time recovery, is used to restore a database to a previous state after data loss, corruption, or failure. It helps you fix problems by rolling back to a known good point. Replication, on the other hand, is about copying and synchronizing data continuously between databases or clusters to ensure high availability, load balancing, or disaster recovery. Replication is useful for keeping standby databases ready to take over if the primary fails, or for scaling read operations. Recovery is used after an incident to restore data, while replication is used proactively to prevent downtime and data loss. In my experience, I have used replication during zero-downtime migrations to keep databases in sync, and recovery methods like PITR to quickly restore service after incidents.

💬 **Question**: What is the difference between a StatefulSet, a DaemonSet, and a Deployment in Kubernetes?

---

⭐️ **Answer**: In Kubernetes, StatefulSet, DaemonSet, and Deployment are different types of controllers used to manage pods, each serving a specific purpose:

- **Deployment**: This is the most common controller used to manage stateless applications. It ensures that a specified number of pod replicas are running and can perform rolling updates and rollbacks. Deployments are ideal for scalable, stateless services where pods are interchangeable.

- **StatefulSet**: This controller manages stateful applications that require stable, unique network identities and persistent storage. Each pod in a StatefulSet has a persistent identifier and storage that remains consistent across restarts. This is useful for databases or other services where data persistence and ordered deployment are important.

- **DaemonSet**: This ensures that a copy of a pod runs on every node (or a subset of nodes) in the cluster. It is typically used for running background tasks like log collection, monitoring agents, or networking components that need to be present on all nodes.

In my experience managing Kubernetes clusters on AWS EKS, I have used Deployments for microservices, StatefulSets for databases and stateful workloads, and DaemonSets for monitoring and security agents to ensure full cluster coverage. This understanding helps maintain reliability and scalability in production environments.
